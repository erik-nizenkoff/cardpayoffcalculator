Methodology

Each month, the calculator computes interest for every active debt using APR divided by 12. It then uses the greater of the issuer-style minimum estimate, the entered minimum payment, or $25, capped at the amount needed to pay the debt off that month.

The issuer-style minimum estimate is current balance times 1% plus that month's interest. The payment first covers interest, then reduces principal. Avalanche applies extra money to the highest APR debt until it is paid, then cascades. Snowball applies extra money to the smallest balance until it is paid, then cascades. Paying minimums only applies no extra payment.

The schedule stops when all balances reach $0 or after 600 months. Results are estimates and do not include new charges, fees, mid-cycle balance transfers, penalty APRs, payment timing differences, or issuer-specific minimum rules beyond the model above. Promotional/intro APRs are supported — enter them per card in the Intro APR fields.

FAQ

What payment do I need to be debt-free by a target month?

Enter a target payoff month. The calculator uses a binary search over monthly payment amounts and reports the smallest estimated payment that pays all entered debts by that month.

What is the debt avalanche method?

Avalanche pays every debt's minimum, then sends extra money to the highest APR debt. It is usually the lowest-interest method.

What is the debt snowball method?

Snowball pays every debt's minimum, then sends extra money to the smallest balance. It may cost more interest, but it can produce a faster first payoff.

Why do minimum payments sometimes show a warning?

If a debt's minimum payment is less than or equal to the monthly interest charge, principal does not shrink. The calculator flags that as a minimum-payment warning.

Can I use this offline?

Yes. This is a single self-contained HTML file with client-side calculations. After the page is loaded or saved, it can run without a network connection.

Debt Avalanche Method

The debt avalanche method pays every minimum first, then sends all extra money to the highest-APR balance. Once that debt is paid off, the freed-up payment rolls to the next-highest APR.

Use avalanche when your main goal is paying the least possible interest and you can stay consistent even if the first payoff takes longer.

Try it in the calculator →

Debt Snowball Method

The debt snowball method pays every minimum first, then targets the smallest balance. It can cost more interest than avalanche, but it often creates a faster first win.

Use snowball when closing accounts quickly helps you keep momentum and stick with the plan.

Paying Off by a Target Month

A target payoff month calculation works backward from your desired debt-free month. The calculator tests monthly payment amounts until it finds the smallest payment that clears all entered balances by that month.

This is most useful when a bonus, refinance deadline, move, or personal milestone gives you a firm payoff window.

The Minimum Payment Trap

Minimum payments can keep an account current while barely reducing principal. If the payment is close to the monthly interest charge, payoff can stretch for years or decades.

Adding even a modest fixed extra payment usually changes the payoff path more than switching methods alone.

About This Calculator

Card Payoff Calculator uses standard amortization math to model how your debt balances shrink over time with different payment strategies.

The avalanche and snowball methods are the two most widely recommended debt payoff strategies by consumer finance educators. This tool lets you compare them side by side alongside your current minimum-payment path so you can see the real cost of each choice.
